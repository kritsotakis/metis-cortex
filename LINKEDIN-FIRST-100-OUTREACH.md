## Why these 100 matter more than any other 100

LinkedIn's algorithm uses your first batch of follower interactions to decide who else sees your page's posts. If your first 100 are service-business owners and Sydney operators, the algorithm pushes future content toward similar people. If your first 100 are recruiters, agencies, and random old contacts, the algorithm thinks the page is for them — and your prospects never see your posts.

Spend the time picking well. The algorithm decides for you for the next 12 months based on this batch.

## You have a 30-day window

LinkedIn cap: **250 invites/month per admin** to follow a Page. You'll only use ~100 of those, but the cap resets monthly so there's no urgency penalty if you spread it across a few sessions.

## Pick from these — high-value targets

**Tier A — Direct ICP** (aim for 50–60 of the 100)

- ✅ **Sydney service business owners** running cleaners / tradies / real estate / dental / aesthetic / allied health / mortgage brokers / financial planners / accountants
- ✅ **Owner-operators of small businesses** (10 employees or fewer is the sweet spot)
- ✅ **Strata managers** in Sydney — DSK serves strata, this is a known-warm category
- ✅ **People who follow / engage with content from Sydney small-business associations** (NSW Small Business Commissioner, Sydney Local Chamber, etc.)

**Tier B — Adjacent / amplifiers** (aim for 30–40 of the 100)

- ✅ **Sydney founders + operators** in any vertical — they understand operator-built businesses, will engage with the content
- ✅ **Australian B2B SaaS founders** — peer founders who'll share the brand-launch story when you post case-study content
- ✅ **Operations-heavy roles** (COO, ops manager, GM) at SMB-scale Australian companies
- ✅ **Australian AI / automation operators** — small group, but they'll engage with substantive technical posts and amplify reach
- ✅ **Sydney-based business journalists / podcasters** if any are in your network — they're discovery accelerants

**Tier C — Personal connections worth keeping in (aim for 10–20 of the 100)**

- ✅ Family + close friends who you'd want to support the launch — they soft-signal the algorithm that the page is "real"
- ✅ Past colleagues from credible roles — adds professional credibility to early follower list

## Pick from these — DO NOT INVITE

**Tier X — these dilute or harm the algorithm signal**

- ❌ **Recruiters** — their feed is already noise; a follow-page invite from you adds to that noise. They won't engage. The algorithm reads no-engagement as "wrong audience."
- ❌ **Other AI agencies / Speed-to-Lead competitors** — direct competitive signal. They'll see your content first, copy what works.
- ❌ **General marketing agencies / business consultants** — adjacent enough to confuse the algorithm into pushing your content to other agencies (who don't buy from you).
- ❌ **Random connections from years ago** — if you wouldn't recognise them at a coffee shop, skip. No-engagement signal again.
- ❌ **Big-corporate employees** (Atlassian / Canva / Telstra etc.) — they don't have buying authority for SMB AI, and the algorithm reads enterprise-followers as "this content is for enterprise" which kills SMB reach.
- ❌ **People whose LinkedIn looks dormant** (no posts in 12+ months) — they won't engage; dead-weight follows that hurt the algorithm.

## The invite blurb

LinkedIn's "Invite to follow Page" custom note caps at ~300 chars. This one fits:

```
I just launched Metis Cortex — done-for-you AI receptionists for Australian
service businesses. Built and proven on my own cleaning business (DSK). If
missed calls are a real number for your business or you know someone who'd
find it useful, would mean a lot if you'd follow the page.
```

**Word count: 47. Character count: ~290.** Within the limit, founder-to-peer tone, no salesy language, has the proof point (DSK), single soft ask.

### When to deviate from the blurb

If you're inviting a personal close contact (family, very close friend), use a more personal version:

```
Just launched my new business. Building AI receptionists for Aussie service
businesses, starting with DSK as the case study. Would mean a lot if you'd
follow the page — first 100 followers shape the LinkedIn algorithm forever.
```

If you're inviting a peer founder you know professionally, use:

```
Launched Metis Cortex this week — AI receptionists for AU service biz.
DSK pilot just kicked off. Would appreciate the follow if you've got
30 seconds. Reciprocate happily on yours.
```

Don't write a custom note for every invite — that's the path to never sending. Use the default blurb for ~85 of the 100, and the personalised versions only for the 15 closest contacts.

## Execution batches

Don't try to invite all 100 in one sitting — LinkedIn's anti-spam systems may rate-limit or temp-ban your account if 100 invites go out in 10 minutes. Spread it.

| Batch | When | Invites |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Day 1 (page launch) | 25 — start with closest network |
| 2 | Day 3 | 25 — Tier A direct ICP |
| 3 | Day 7 | 25 — Tier B adjacent |
| 4 | Day 12 | 25 — fill in remaining ICP candidates you missed |
| | | **100 total over 12 days** |

This pacing reads as natural human activity to LinkedIn, not bot/spam.

## After the first 100 are following

- Stop inviting. The algorithm has its signal.
- Don't post yet — wait for the DSK pilot data to land. The first post should be the case study, not a "we're launching" post.
- Engage with content from the people you invited — like, comment, share. Recprical engagement strengthens the algorithm's read of your audience.
- The next 100 (Days 30–60) come organically through that engagement + the eventual case-study post.
